Smart Shower Accessibility Guidelines for the Ageing Market

From discovery research to expert validation, this MSc thesis project produced actionable design principles for inclusive shower interfaces, addressing the growing needs of older adults in connected home products.

  • Role: UX Researcher
  • Timeline: Jan – Sept 2025
  • Location: Goldsmiths, University of London
  • Tools: Figma · Miro . Solidworks . KeyShot . Arduino

Overview

As the global population ages, ensuring that smart technologies address the unique needs of older adults becomes increasingly essential. This project focuses on developing user-centered design guidelines for smart shower systems that improve accessibility, safety, and independence for senior users.

Research Objectives

The aim was to explore how multimodal interactions in smart shower systems (e.g., touchscreens, voice commands, and gesture controls) can enhance usability for older adults while addressing their physical and cognitive challenges.

Methodology: Mixed Methods Approach

I followed a comparative, evidence‑led process from interviews → prototyping → expert review → actionable guidelines.

83% task completion, 17% error rate, expert‑validated.

Background Research:

  • Reviewed 6 academic studies and conducted qualitative content analysis
  • Identified key physical and cognitive challenges

● User Research

Participants:

8 older adults (60+) and carers, selected to represent a range of mobility levels, cognitive abilities, and familiarity with technology. Participants were recruited through local community centres, Goldsmiths faculty connections, and caregiving networks to ensure diverse perspectives on accessibility needs.

Interview Agenda:
  • Current shower habits and setup
  • Physical and cognitive challenges during use
  • Opinions on smart shower controls (touch, gesture, voice)
  • Suggestions for safer, more accessible designs
Key Insights:
  • Falls, fatigue, and mobility limitations were the most common physical challenges
  • Memory issues and fear of new technology reduced willingness to try smart features1
  • Touchscreens were preferred over gesture and voice, provided buttons and sliders were large and easy to read
  • High demand for visible safety features, including temperature lock and emergency controls
  • Prototyped 3 interaction methods: touchscreen, gesture, and voice
  • Concept testing done with 6 older adults using real-world tasks (e.g. adjusting water temperature)
  • Touchscreen was preferred; 83% task completion vs. gesture (50%) and voice (17%)

Design Iterations

Second Iteration:

Based on participant feedback, the touchscreen wireframe was redesigned on an iPad Mini (Unlike the first version, which was designed at mobile size) to improve readability, element size and add options for selecting water outlets, preparing it for expert evaluation.

Expert Evaluation:

Preliminary design guidelines were developed from user research and divided into two categories:

  • Must-Have Features: Touchscreen interaction, large and simple interface, visual feedback, and essential safety functions
  • Nice-to-Have Features: Customisable water outlets, light indicators for temperature and volume, smartphone app control, and personalised user profiles

The second touchscreen prototype and design guidelines were reviewed by 5 experts in UX design, industrial design, and mechanical engineering, providing validation and suggestions for refinement.

Third Iteration:

Based on expert feedback, the touchscreen prototype was refined with larger sliders, clearer icons, and an updated layout featuring a smaller title, relocated settings icon, and a new profile button for personalisation.

ّFinal Guidelines (Titles only)

The final guidelines, shaped by both user and expert feedback, aim to support the design of accessible, safe, and intuitive smart shower systems. They are divided into “Must-Have” features for core usability and safety, and “Nice-to-Have” features that enhance user satisfaction but may be optional to reduce costs.

Must-Have Features Nice-to-Have Features
Touchscreen interaction Customisable water outlet options
Large, high-contrast interface Light indicators for temperature and volume
Visual and haptic feedback Smartphone app remote control
Safety features (e.g. anti-scald, IP65 rating) Personalised user profiles

Impact and Applications

The guidelines developed in this research aim to bridge the gap in the market by providing practical solutions for designing inclusive smart shower systems. This work has potential implications for developing other smart home technologies for senior users, promoting safety, comfort, and independence.
